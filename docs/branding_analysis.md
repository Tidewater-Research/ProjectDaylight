## Understanding Your User

### Who They Are

Your primary user is a parent in an active custody dispute who needs to:
- Document incidents as they happen (often while exhausted or emotional)
- Organize evidence scattered across texts, emails, photos, and verbal interactions
- Create court-ready materials that lawyers and judges can use
- Track patterns of behavior over time
- Prepare for GAL (Guardian ad Litem) interviews and court appearances

### Their Emotional State

This is critical for branding. Your user is typically:

| State | Implication for Branding |
|-------|-------------------------|
| **Exhausted** | Need simplicity, not complexity |
| **Anxious** | Need calm reassurance, not urgency |
| **Overwhelmed** | Need "one thing at a time," not feature overload |
| **Defensive** | Need neutral language, not combative framing |
| **Scared** | Need strength, not sympathy |
| **Isolated** | Need professional support, not community |

### What They're Searching For

Likely Google searches:
- "how to document custody issues"
- "evidence for custody case"
- "app to track co-parent behavior"
- "custody journal app"
- "document incidents for court"
- "voice recorder for custody"

### The Unspoken Need

Your user doesn't just need a toolâ€”they need **credibility**. When they walk into court, they want to look like someone who has their life together, who is organized, who is reasonable. The app should make them *feel* more capable and prepared.

## Visual Identity Considerations

### Color Psychology for Legal/Evidence Apps

| Color | Association | Use Case |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Navy/Dark Blue** | Trust, authority, professionalism | Primary brand color |
| **White/Off-white** | Clarity, cleanliness, truth | Backgrounds |
| **Warm Gray** | Neutrality, sophistication | Secondary elements |
| **Amber/Gold** | Warmth, optimism (subtle) | Accents only |
| **Avoid: Red** | Anger, conflict, danger | Wrong tone entirely |
| **Avoid: Purple** | Creative, mystical | Too playful |
| **Avoid: Bright colors** | Childish, unserious | Wrong context |

### Typography Recommendations

- **Headlines:** Strong, professional serif OR clean geometric sans
- **Body:** Highly legible sans-serif (system fonts work)
- **Avoid:** Trendy/stylized fonts, script fonts, anything "fun"

### Logo Direction

Given the subject matter, the logo should be:
- **Simple:** Icon + wordmark, not complex illustration
- **Geometric:** Clean shapes (circle, square, simple lines)
- **Symbolic:** Could incorporate: light/sun rays, timeline, document, shield

**Avoid:**
- Gavel imagery (too clichÃ©, aggressive)
- Family/parent imagery (too specific, could feel exclusionary)
- Lock/shield (too security-app)
- Scales of justice (too legal-firm)

## Pitfalls to Avoid

### 1. **The "Warrior Parent" Trap** ðŸš«

**The temptation:** Position as a weapon to "win" custody battles.

**Why it fails:**
- Courts view combative parents negatively
- Attracts wrong user psychology
- Legal liability concerns
- Alienates attorneys who might recommend

**Instead:** Position as organization and documentation, not warfare.

---

### 2. **The "Sympathy" Trap** ðŸš«

**The temptation:** Lean into emotional support messaging ("We're here for you in this difficult time").

**Why it fails:**
- Users need strength, not pity
- Comes across as patronizing
- Doesn't differentiate from therapy apps
- Undercuts professional credibility

**Instead:** Acknowledge difficulty briefly, then focus on empowerment through organization.

---

### 3. **The "Legal Advice" Trap** ðŸš«

**The temptation:** Use terminology that implies legal guidance ("Protect your rights," "Build your case").

**Why it fails:**
- Legal liability (unauthorized practice of law)
- Overpromises what app can deliver
- Could harm users who rely on it instead of attorneys

**Instead:** Focus on documentation and organization. Let lawyers interpret.

---

### 4. **The "Victim" Trap** ðŸš«

**The temptation:** Market to "victims" of bad co-parents, abusive situations.

**Why it fails:**
- Creates adversarial positioning
- May attract users for wrong reasons
- Could be used for false allegations
- Reputational risk

**Instead:** Neutral positioning around documentation and preparation.

---

### 5. **The "Feature Overload" Trap** ðŸš«

**The temptation:** Market every AI feature, every capability.

**Why it fails:**
- Overwhelms exhausted users
- Dilutes core value proposition
- Sounds like tech, not solution

**Instead:** Lead with outcomes ("court-ready timeline"), not features ("AI transcription").

---

### 6. **The "Custody Only" Trap** ðŸš«

**The temptation:** Focus branding entirely on custody cases.

**Why it fails:**
- Limits market size
- Same tool could help with:
  - Workplace documentation
  - Medical history tracking
  - Elder care documentation
  - Any legal dispute preparation

**Instead:** Lead with custody (it's the highest-pain market) but keep brand expandable.

---

Examples of what to avoid:
- **"CustodyWin"** â€” Too aggressive, overpromises

## Recommended Direction

### Primary Recommendation: Stick with "Daylight"

**Rationale:**
1. Already built into codebase and mental model
2. Metaphorically strong (truth, clarity, bringing to light)
3. Doesn't pigeonhole to custody-only
4. Professional without being cold
5. Hopeful without being naive
6. Short, memorable, spellable

**Required changes:**
- Drop "Project" from all marketing
- Develop strong tagline to clarify purpose
- Build visual identity around "light" metaphor
- Secure domains: daylight.app, getdaylight.com, etc.

### Tagline Options (Ranked)

1. **"From chaos to clarity"** â€” Best overall, captures transformation
2. **"Your story, documented"** â€” Personal, emphasizes user's narrative
3. **"Court-ready documentation"** â€” Direct but less emotional
4. **"Organize your truth"** â€” Good, might feel slightly confrontational
5. **"Bring everything to light"** â€” Plays on name, but vague
